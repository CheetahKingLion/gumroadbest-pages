When Mat Dryhurst discusses AI, protocols, and the future of art—as he did recently in Sleek Magazine—most creators treat it like abstract philosophy. They focus on decentralization and internet architecture, nod along, and then go right back to the same trap: trying to out-hustle the algorithm.

Here is the reality: Tools don’t generate income; packaging a measurable outcome does.

While the rest of the industry argues over who owns the pixels, smart operators are building the pipes. If you are a freelancer or a solo service provider, Dryhurst’s focus on the underlying "protocols" of AI isn't just theory—it’s the most practical business pivot you can make right now. Success isn't about the AI model; it’s about the governance of the output.

What most people miss

Most people chase the hype—using the latest generative model to create "cool" content—because it feels like being on the cutting edge. They are wrong. Real money is made by applying that tech to a specific, painful bottleneck.

When you pitch a client by saying, "I can use AI to make art," you’ve just branded yourself a commodity. You are now competing with mid-tier models and teenagers with a subscription. But when you pitch, "I can implement a proprietary protocol that cuts your post-production time by 60% while locking in your brand's visual consistency," you’ve pivoted to a consultant. You aren't just using a tool; you're acting as a systems architect.

As explored in The Trust-Creator-AI Triad: Why Your Service Business Is Currently Over-Tooling and Under-Selling, the market is bloated with "tool-first" thinkers. They are drowning in subscription fees and struggling to land high-ticket clients. Dryhurst’s point is structural: Clients don't care about your prompt engineering skills. They care about the predictability of the result.

Field note

I’ve watched too many freelancers lose contracts by pitching "AI integration" as a futuristic novelty. It backfired every time. Clients don't buy sci-fi automation; they buy time back, fewer revisions, and the ability to get projects past their own internal stakeholders.

Your value isn't the AI—it’s the "hardened" workflow. You want a system so robust that the client doesn't need to know how the machine works; they only need to know that the work arrives on time and on brand. Generic advice is easy to ignore. A proven before-and-after workflow improvement is impossible to argue with. If you can show a client their current cycle takes 10 days and your protocol drops it to 3, the fee is no longer the point of contention.
