News broke recently via The Futurum Group regarding Adobe’s strategic interest in Topaz Labs. If you’re an internet entrepreneur or freelancer, your instinct might be to worry about Adobe jamming more 'AI features' into your Creative Cloud subscription.

Forget the worry. Start auditing your bank account instead.

Most creators are focused on the wrong target: the tools themselves. They view AI as a shortcut to get faster at commodity tasks—editing a clip, color-correcting, upscaling an image. They assume that if they’re 10% faster, they can handle 10% more clients. That’s a one-way ticket to burnout.

The Adobe-Topaz move confirms that high-quality, AI-assisted output is the new baseline. When the floor rises, the value of manual "pixel pushing" drops. You aren't competing with other humans anymore; you’re competing with an algorithm that’s being baked into the industry standard.

What most people miss

Most creators see this news and think they need to master a new plugin. They miss the real shift: Adobe is commoditizing the process, not the problem.

As I’ve covered in my research on Why Mat Dryhurst’s AI Protocol Theory Is Your New Competitive Moat, the people making real money today aren't just "using" the latest AI. They control the protocol of the deliverable. If you’re still selling "video editing," you’re selling a commodity. If you’re selling "automated, high-fidelity brand assets that cut internal marketing turnaround by 70%," you’re selling a system.

Tools don't create income; packaging a measurable outcome does. The Adobe-Topaz integration will make cleanup and upscaling trivial. If your entire business model depends on doing that manual work, your margins are about to get crushed.

Field note

In my years working with agency owners and creators, I’ve seen a clear pattern: Clients don't buy automation because it sounds futuristic. They buy it when it kills their bottlenecks, stops the revision cycle, or helps them close deals faster.

I once watched a creator struggle to sell $500 "AI-enhanced" videos. He was pitching the tech. He pivoted to pitching a "24-Hour Brand Authority System." He used the exact same tools, but changed the offer. He started charging $3,000 a month because he framed the output as a way for the client to capture market share.

  • The Commodity Approach: "I use the latest AI to make your video look sharper."
  • The Differentiated Approach: "I provide a turn-key content engine that eliminates your bottleneck in producing daily high-fidelity social assets."

How to turn this into an offer this week

If you want to capitalize on this trend, stop acting like an editor and start acting like a systems engineer. Here is your plan for the next seven days:

  1. Identify the Bottleneck: Ask your client, "What part of your creative workflow keeps you up at night?" Is it the lag between filming and posting? Inconsistent brand quality? The high cost of production houses?
  2. Define the Outcome: Stop promising "better video." Promise "a 48-hour delivery window for high-fidelity brand assets, driven by a proprietary, automated workflow."
  3. Standardize Your Stack: Fold tools like Topaz-style AI upscaling into a repeatable, internal system. Your client doesn't need a tutorial on your tools; they need to know your system is reliable.

Final Thoughts: The Speed of Implementation

Adobe is betting that the future of creativity is integrated. They’re stripping away the friction. Your job isn't to compete with their integration, but to layer your own "human-in-the-loop" expertise on top of it.

If you can take raw, high-effort content and pass it through a system that makes it look polished and brand-aligned in a fraction of the time, you have a business. If you just learn the "latest Adobe feature," you have a hobby that’s about to be automated away.

The fastest path to revenue is selling a repeatable workflow. Build the system, sell the outcome, and leave the tool-chasing to the amateurs.
