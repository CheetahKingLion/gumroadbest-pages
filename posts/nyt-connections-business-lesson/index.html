Millions of people play Connections every day. As noted in recent analysis from TechRadar, the game is a masterclass in taxonomy. But if you are a freelancer or a solo entrepreneur, you shouldn't just be playing for the dopamine hit. You should be studying it as a business development exercise.

The game forces you to take 16 disparate items and sort them into four logical buckets. This is exactly what your clients struggle to do with their own operations. They’re drowning in tools, pain points, and potential outcomes, but they have no framework to organize them. If you can bridge that gap, you stop being a contractor and start being a necessity.

The Pattern Recognition Gap

Most freelancers operate like a junk drawer. They list "content creation," "social media management," and "data entry" as if those services mean anything to a CFO. When you approach a prospect with a list of disjointed services, you’re forcing them to do the work. You’re asking them to play the game of Connections on their own dime, hoping they can figure out how your skills fit into their growth strategy.

That’s a losing game. High-value earners do the opposite: they present the completed board. They show the client exactly how their specific workflow solves the company’s immediate bottleneck. As I discussed in The Trust-Creator-AI Triad: Why Your Service Business Is Currently Over-Tooling and Under-Selling, the market is saturated with people who know how to use tools but have no clue how to build a trust-based system. Stop selling the tool and start selling the bucket.

What most people miss

The money isn't in the newest prompt-engineering hack or the latest all-in-one platform. Tools don't create income; packaging a measurable outcome does.

The popularity of Connections proves that people are starving for clarity amidst chaos. Clients don't want more tools; they want their chaos categorized. If you take their unorganized content production and turn it into a consistent 48-hour delivery cycle, you aren't just a service provider anymore. You’re an operating system.

Field note

I’ve spent the last six months auditing service businesses, and the pattern is consistent: the "commodity" providers show off their tool stack. The high-earners show off their turnaround time.

Clients don't buy automation because it sounds futuristic. They buy it because it cuts turnaround, reduces revisions, and helps them move faster. If your pitch is "I use AI to generate videos," you’re a commodity. If your pitch is "I have a workflow that cuts your video production cycle from 10 days to 24 hours," you’re an investment. The stack only matters after the buyer understands the business outcome.

How to turn this into an offer this week

If you want to capitalize on the need for structured, outcome-based work, stop guessing what the client wants. Stop building from scratch. Use a system that already works, plug it into the client's specific bottleneck, and charge for the speed and sanity you provide.
