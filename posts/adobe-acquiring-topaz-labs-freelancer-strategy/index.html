The news is out: Adobe is acquiring Topaz Labs. To most, it’s just another headline in a crowded feed. If you’re a freelancer, read it as a wake-up call to stop playing small.

When giants like Adobe swallow specialized tools, the market value of those standalone features drops to zero. If you’ve built a business model around selling "AI upscaling" or "AI restoration," a single software update just vaporized your edge. As I broke down in Adobe’s Topaz Deal: Why Your 'AI Stack' Is Killing Your Margins (And What To Do Instead), the "tool-first" freelancer is an endangered species.

What most people miss

Most freelancers think chasing every new AI tool keeps them ahead. They spend weekends geeking out over diffusion models and upscalers, convinced that being "first" to a tool translates to more leads.

They’re wrong.

Tools don’t create income; solving a measurable problem does. Once Topaz Labs becomes a native button in Premiere or After Effects, your "specialized" service is a commodity. The real money isn't in the tool—it’s in owning the bottleneck. If your client is a production house drowning in a 72-hour turnaround for legacy footage, they don't care about your Topaz proficiency. They care that you can get their content live for a launch in under 24 hours. The tool is just a lever; the revenue is in the speed of the output.

Field note

I’ve watched agencies bleed margins because they lead with their "AI advantage" rather than a business outcome. Clients don't buy automation because it’s shiny or futuristic. They buy it when it cuts turnaround times, stops them from dealing with endless revisions, or helps them win.

When I sit down with a client, I never lead with my tech stack. I lead with the friction: "How much are you losing in ad spend while waiting for these assets to be finalized?"

Generic buzzwords get ignored. Concrete, before-and-after workflow shifts get remembered. When you prove your specific workflow kills a recurring friction point, you stop being a cost center and start being a profit engine. A high-leverage offer will always crush a clever tool stack.

How to turn this into an offer this week

If you want to survive the Adobe-Topaz integration, you need to pivot your service immediately. Here is the framework:

  1. Identify the bottleneck: Look at your client list. What is the one task they constantly complain about? Slow delivery? Poor source files?
  2. Productize the fix: Stop selling "editing." Start selling "The 24-Hour Premium Refresh."
  3. Use the stack as a backend: Use Adobe’s new features to slash your manual labor, but keep your premium pricing. You aren't charging for your hours; you're charging for the time you save them.
  4. Package it: Kill the hourly rate. Create a standardized deliverable that solves a high-stakes problem.

To pull this off, you need to pitch with authority. In the video space, that means having a professional, repeatable workflow you can deploy the moment they say "yes."

The Commodity vs. The System

  • The Commodity Approach: "I use the latest AI upscaling and video tools to make your footage look better." (Client thinks: Adobe will do this for me next month, why pay you?)
  • The Differentiated Approach: "I have a workflow that cuts your production cycle by 60%, allowing you to test 3x more ads every week without burning your budget." (Client thinks: How soon can we start?)

The fastest path to revenue isn't building a platform or chasing a viral trend. It’s selling a repeatable workflow your clients actually value. Adobe is doing the heavy lifting by integrating the tech. Your job is to focus on the business—the revenue, the deadlines, and the growth—and use that tech to make their results inevitable.
